Global Warming

The phenomenon of global warming is when radiation emitted by the sun is absorbed by the earth. This radiation absorbed by the earth is then emitted back outwards causing the surface of the earth to warm. When the radiation emitted by earth tries to go back to space, it is trapped by greenhouse gasses in earth's atmosphere that in turn send radiation back to earth's surface causing it to perpetually warm.

The world is already seeing adverse effects of the heat, for instance, birds in North America.

Every spring in the northern hemisphere, birds make the trek north to acheive resources like food and shelter that springtime usually offers. When winter rolls around again and it gets colder up north, birds make the trek south again to acheive a similar climate to what they are accustomed to.

Here is where a variety of species could be seen 40 years ago in the winter.

And here is where they can be seen today.

Since surface temperatures have been increasing the past 40 years, locations further north are now warmer then they would have been 40 so years ago. As a result, many birds end up staying further north than they did 40 years ago because the warmer temperatures is what they are used to.

While this may seem harmless, when birds return south but find themselves in an unfamiliar location, the terrain is not always similar to what they are accustomed to. Because of this, many birds die struggling to find food and shelter in such an unfamiliar location. Additionally, as temperatures continue to warm, who knows how far north birds may find themselves in the future and the effect the absence of birds has the on their ecosystems is unknown.

If we continue to produce carbon at this scale, by the end of the century, the average surface temperature could raise by 3 degrees Celsius. The implications this has on people and biology are very serious and unpredictable.
